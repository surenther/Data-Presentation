Executive Summary¶

The objective of this analysis is to evaluate the performance of Netflix content across three distinct dimensions: Global Viewership Trends, Country-Specific Popularity, and All-Time Top Performers.

As Netflix transitions into a more globalized production model, we are analyzing these datasets to identify if "Local-for-Global" (non-English) content provides a higher Return on Investment (ROI) and better subscriber retention than traditional domestic English content.

Call to Action: Based on the data, we are proposing a 20% budget reallocation toward non-English episodic series for the 2027 fiscal year to capture high-growth international markets.

The Global Attention Span¶

Before diving into specific shows, we must understand the "health" of the platform. By plotting Weekly Total Hours Viewed, we determine if Netflix’s global audience engagement is growing, shrinking, or cyclical. This sets the stage for our "Call to Action" by proving there is a massive, hungry audience waiting for content.

In [14]:
import pandas as pd
import seaborn as sns
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Aggregate hours by week
weekly_engagement = df_global.groupby('week')['weekly_hours_viewed'].sum().reset_index()

plt.figure(figsize=(12, 6))
sns.lineplot(data=weekly_engagement, x='week', y='weekly_hours_viewed', color='#E50914')
plt.title('Global Weekly Viewership Trends', fontsize=15)
plt.ylabel('Total Hours Viewed (Billions)')
plt.grid(axis='y', linestyle='--', alpha=0.7)
plt.show()

Key Observations from the Data:¶

  • Engagement Scale: The platform consistently maintains a massive baseline, with global viewership rarely dipping below 500 million hours per week.

  • Peak Volatility: The data shows extreme "High-Engagement Events" where viewership spikes by over 100% (surpassing 1.2 billion hours) in a very short window. This proves that the global audience is capable of rapid, massive mobilization.

  • Cyclical Nature: There is a visible "ebb and flow" to the data. Viewership does not grow in a straight line; rather, it moves in cycles of high intensity followed by periods of "cooling off" or baseline consumption.

  • Long-term Stability: Despite the massive spikes, the "floor" of the data (the lowest points) has remained relatively stable from 2021 to 2024, indicating a loyal and permanent global user base.

Summary: This visual proves that Netflix possesses a high-capacity global distribution engine. We have identified that the audience is not static; they respond to "events" with massive surges in watch time. This visual confirms that the infrastructure and the audience are ready for high-volume consumption—the next sections will help us determine which types of content are most effective at triggering these massive surges

Content Stickiness¶

This visualization helps us categorize our content into two types: "The Sprint" (shows that hit huge numbers for 1-2 weeks and vanish) and "The Marathon" (shows that stay in the Top 10 for months). For a subscription business like Netflix, "The Marathon" shows are often more valuable because they keep users coming back week after week, reducing the likelihood of them canceling their subscription.

Key Observations from the Data:¶

  • The "Launch Peak" Phenomenon: The highest viewership spikes occur almost exclusively within the first 1–4 weeks. Engagement is highest when the content is fresh, with some titles reaching massive heights of nearly 600 million hours in a single week.

  • Rapid Viewership Decay: There is a sharp downward "slope" in the data. After week 5, the density of high-hour titles drops significantly. Very few titles can maintain more than 100 million hours per week after their initial month on the platform.

  • The 10-Week "Stamina" Barrier: There is a clear "long tail" of content that stays in the Top 10 for 10 to 30 weeks. While these titles have lower weekly velocity (usually under 50 million hours), their cumulative contribution to platform "stickiness" is immense.

  • Exceptional Outliers: The rare data points appearing at the 20+ week mark with steady viewership represent the "top-tier" assets that provide the highest long-term value for subscriber retention.

Summary: This visuals proves that longevity is the rarest commodity on the platform. While we are highly successful at launching content with high initial velocity, the data concludes that very few titles possess the "stamina" to stay relevant beyond 10 weeks. For an executive, this justifies a strategy that prioritizes content depth and replayability to stretch those dots further to the right of the chart.

The "Hit-Maker" Pareto¶

In business, the Pareto Principle (80/20 rule) often suggests that 80% of results come from 20% of efforts. By analyzing the views_first_91_days from our most popular titles, we can show that a handful of blockbuster titles carry the platform's reputation and viewership load. This justifies the massive investment required for high-quality global originals.

# Sort the data by views
df_popular_sorted = df_popular.sort_values('views_first_91_days', ascending=False).reset_index(drop=True)

# Calculate the cumulative percentage of total views
df_popular_sorted['cumulative_percent'] = 100 * (df_popular_sorted['views_first_91_days'].cumsum() / df_popular_sorted['views_first_91_days'].sum())

fig, ax1 = plt.subplots(figsize=(14, 8))

# Bar Chart 
ax1.bar(df_popular_sorted['show_title'][:10], df_popular_sorted['views_first_91_days'][:10], color='#221F1F', alpha=0.8)
ax1.set_ylabel('Total Views (First 91 Days)', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax1.set_xlabel('Content Title', fontsize=12)
ax1.tick_params(axis='x', rotation=45)

# Cumulative Line Chart
ax2 = ax1.twinx()
ax2.plot(df_popular_sorted['show_title'][:10], df_popular_sorted['cumulative_percent'][:10], color='#E50914', marker='o', linewidth=2.5, label='Cumulative %')
ax2.set_ylabel('Cumulative Percentage of Total Views (%)', fontsize=12, color='#E50914', fontweight='bold')
ax2.set_ylim(0, 110)

# Add a threshold line at 80%
ax2.axhline(y=80, color='grey', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5)

plt.title('The Pareto of Popularity: Top Titles Driving Massive Platform Value', fontsize=16, fontweight='bold')
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Key Observations from the Data:¶

  • The "Mega-Hit" Gap: There is a significant viewership drop-off between the top three titles (Squid Game, Wednesday, and Red Notice) and the rest of the list. These "Top 3" titles are in a league of their own, generating nearly double the views of titles further down the list.

  • Non-English Dominance (Again): The #1 title driving this entire chart is Squid Game. This reinforces the conclusion from Section 2: our single most valuable asset was produced outside of Hollywood, proving the "Local-for-Global" strategy works at the highest level.

  • The Cumulative Climb: The red line shows that even just these top 10 titles alone account for a massive, disproportionate percentage of the platform's "All-Time" popularity. This confirms that the audience isn't just watching anything; they are gravitating toward these massive, cultural "events."

  • Reliability of English IP: While Non-English leads, English-language "tentpoles" (like Stranger Things and Red Notice) provide the "middle-of-the-pack" stability that keeps the cumulative total climbing steadily.

Summary: This visuals proves that Netflix success is driven by a handful of outliers. The data concludes that a single "Mega-Hit" like Squid Game provides more platform value than dozens of smaller releases combined. This is the ultimate evidence for our Call to Action: we must focus our 2027 budget on high-quality, culturally significant "Big Bets"—specifically in the international episodic category—to capture the next generation of platform-defining hits.
